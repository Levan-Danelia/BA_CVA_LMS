<h1>Standalone CVA (SCVA)</h1>

<!-- 1. SUMMARY & STRUCTURE -->

<h2>Summary</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>The <b>Standalone CVA (SCVA)</b> is the primary building block of the Basic Approach (BA-CVA) framework as defined under <b>PRA PS9/24</b>. It represents the specific regulatory capital charge for a single counterparty <i>c</i>, calculated by aggregating the risk profiles of all underlying netting sets.</p>

<p>This technical summary explores the "engine room" of the CVA calculation, where supervisory risk weights (\(RW_c\)), effective maturities (\(M_{NS}\)), and Exposure at Default (\(EAD_{NS}\)) converge. By removing legacy floors and standardizing the risk-weighting process, the SCVA provides a granular, risk-sensitive foundation for the broader portfolio aggregation and hedging recognition that follows.</p>

</div>

<!-- 2. DEFINITION OF KEY TERMS -->

<h2>Definition of Key Terms</h2>

<p>The calculation of Standalone CVA (SCVA) is governed by a prescriptive formula that translates regulatory inputs into a capital charge. Understanding the conceptual role of each variable is essential for accurate implementation under the PRA framework.</p>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ SCVA_c = \frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot RW_c \cdot \sum_{NS \in c} (M_{NS} \cdot EAD_{NS} \cdot DF_{NS}) \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Alpha (\(\alpha\))</span>

<p>A supervisory scalar designed to align the Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) exposure values with the CVA framework. Conceptually, it acts as a neutralizer to prevent the double-counting of conservatism already present in general exposure calculations. For specific entity types, such as pension funds, this scalar is adjusted to provide capital relief reflecting their unique risk profiles.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Risk Weight (\(RW_c\))</span>

<p>A prescribed weight that reflects the perceived volatility of a counterparty's credit spread. This variable is the primary driver of risk differentiation in the formula, determined by a mapping process that considers both the economic sector and the credit quality of the counterparty.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Effective Maturity (\(M_{NS}\))</span>

<p>The time horizon of the risk for a given netting set. This component measures the duration of the exposure, with the framework applying specific rules for how maturity is capped or floored depending on the collateralization status of the transactions.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Exposure at Default (\(EAD_{NS}\))</span>

<p>The potential exposure value of the netting set at the time of a hypothetical default. This input is typically sourced from the firm's existing credit risk engines and represents the "size" of the risk being capitalized.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Discount Factor (\(DF_{NS}\))</span>

<p>A factor used to adjust the exposure to its present value. It ensures that the capital charge appropriately reflects the time value of money, applying a standardized supervisory discount rate to all future exposures.</p>

</div>

<!-- 4. EFFECTIVE MATURITY -->

<h2>Effective Maturity</h2>

<p>The <span class="highlight-blue">Effective Maturity (\(M_{NS}\))</span> provides the critical time dimension within the SCVA formula. It serves as a linear scaler, where longer-dated exposures attract higher capital charges to reflect the increased probability of credit spread volatility over time. The calculation logic for \(M_{NS}\) is split based on the firm's technical methodology and the specific nature of the transactions.</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Derivatives: IMM vs. SA-CCR</span>

<p>For derivative netting sets, the maturity calculation is aligned with the firm's Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) framework:</p>

<ul>

<li><b>IMM Firms:</b> Institutions using the Internal Model Method calculate \(M_{NS}\) based on modeled expected exposure profiles, allowing for a risk-sensitive, time-weighted view of the portfolio's duration.</li>

<li><b>SA-CCR (Non-IMM) Firms:</b> Firms calculate a notional-weighted average remaining maturity. Each transaction's time to expiry is weighted by its notional value relative to the total netting set.</li>

</ul>

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Securities Financing Transactions (SFTs)</span>

<p>SFTs, such as Repurchase Agreements, are included in the CVA scope if they are fair-valued and the risk is material. Because these trades are typically short-term, the rules permit a highly sensitive treatment. Notably, short-dated SFTs benefit directly from the removal of artificial duration floors, ensuring that overnight or weekly financing does not attract a disproportionately high capital charge.</p>

</div>

<p>The PRA Rulebook prescribes specific limits to ensure the risk-sensitivity of the framework while maintaining a prudent boundary for the duration input.</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Caps and Floors</span>

<ul>

<li><b>Floor Removal:</b> A vital technical relief in the BA-CVA is that the 1-year maturity floor <b>does not apply</b> to transactions that are fully collateralized or margined. This allows short-term risk to be capitalized at its actual duration.</li>

<li><b>Contractual Cap:</b> For the purposes of SCVA, the maturity is capped at the <b>longest contractual remaining maturity</b> in the netting set. This ensures that the capital charge accurately accounts for the full lifespan of long-dated contracts, such as 20 or 30-year interest rate swaps.</li>

</ul>

</div>

<!-- 5. DISCOUNT FACTOR -->

<h2>Discount Factor</h2>

<p>The <span class="highlight-blue">Discount Factor (\(DF_{NS}\))</span> is a fundamental parameter used to adjust the future exposure of a netting set to its present value. Conceptually, it ensures that the capital requirement accounts for the time value of money over the duration of the counterparty credit risk. Without this adjustment, long-dated exposures would be disproportionately penalized relative to their economic present value.</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Methodological Divergence: IMM vs. Non-IMM</span>

<p>The PRA Rulebook prescribes different treatments for the discount factor based on the firm's approved methodology for Counterparty Credit Risk:</p>

<ul>

<li><b>Internal Model Method (IMM):</b> For firms with IMM permission, the regulatory text simplifies the input by fixing \(DF_{NS}\) at <b>1.0</b>. This is permitted because IMM exposure profiles (Expected Exposure) are typically already time-weighted or constructed in a manner that implicitly accounts for discounting.</li>

<li><b>Non-IMM Firms:</b> Firms utilizing standardized methods (such as SA-CCR) do not have modeled discounting. Therefore, they must apply a prescriptive supervisory formula to calculate the factor for each netting set.</li>

</ul>

</div>

<h3>The Supervisory Formula</h3>

<p>For non-IMM netting sets, the discount factor is derived using a standardized supervisory rate of 5%. The formula is defined as:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ DF_{NS} = \frac{1 - e^{-0.05 \cdot M_{NS}}}{0.05 \cdot M_{NS}} \]

</div>

<p>This exponential decay function ensures that as the effective maturity (\(M_{NS}\)) increases, the discount factor decreases, appropriately lowering the present value of very long-dated exposure profiles.</p>

<!-- 6. EXPOSURE AT DEFAULT -->

<h2>Exposure at Default (EAD)</h2>

<p>While Risk Weights and Maturity define the intensity and duration of the risk, the <span class="highlight-blue">Exposure at Default (\(EAD_{NS}\))</span> represents the "quantum" of the risk itself. In the context of CVA, EAD serves as the volume-based driver, quantifying the potential loss the firm would face if the counterparty were to default at a specific point in time.</p>

<p>The SCVA does not require a new or separate exposure calculation engine. Instead, it leverages the values already produced by a firm's existing Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) framework. This ensures consistency across the regulatory report: whether a firm utilizes the Standardised Approach (SA-CCR) or the Internal Model Method (IMM), the EAD used for determining credit risk capital also feeds directly into the CVA calculation.</p>

<p>From a conceptual standpoint, the EAD represents the sum of the current replacement cost—the current market value of the trades—and the potential future exposure (PFE). The PFE acts as a statistical add-on, reflecting how future market movements might increase the value of the exposure before a default event is resolved and the positions are closed out.</p>

<!-- 7. ALPHA -->

<h2>Alpha</h2>

<p>The <span class="highlight-blue">Alpha (\(\alpha\))</span> parameter serves as a crucial calibration scalar in the SCVA formula. Its primary purpose is to act as a "neutralizer" between the Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) framework and the CVA framework. Because the CCR exposure values (EAD) typically include a built-in multiplier to account for model risk and general correlation, the SCVA formula divides by \(\alpha\) to strip away this external conservatism before applying CVA-specific risk weights.</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">The "Neutralizer" Logic</span>

<p>You may notice that as the value of \(\alpha\) increases in the denominator, the resulting capital charge decreases. This is mathematically correct because the term \(1 / \alpha\) is designed to <b>reverse</b> the inflation applied during the exposure calculation. For a standard bank, the input EAD is 1.4 times larger than the raw exposure; dividing by 1.4 in the CVA formula simply returns the risk to its baseline value.</p>

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Why Pension Funds see a "Reduction"</span>

<p>The benefit for Pension Funds is realized at the very start of the process. In the CCR framework, their exposures are not subject to the standard 1.4 multiplier. Because they enter the CVA engine with a "leaner" exposure profile (using \(\alpha = 1.0\)), their total capital requirement is naturally lower than a financial counterparty with an identical trade volume. The relief isn't in the division—it's in the fact that there was no inflation to begin with.</p>

</div>

<h2>Standalone CVA (SCVA)</h2>

<p>The <b>Standalone CVA (SCVA)</b> represents the final culmination of our technical deep-dive. Conceptually, it is the regulatory capital requirement for a specific counterparty, calculated as if that counterparty existed in isolation. By multiplying the risk-weighted exposure by the time-dimension and then neutralizing the general credit risk buffer, we arrive at a precise, risk-sensitive measure of credit valuation adjustment risk.</p>

<h3>The Integration Formula</h3>

<p>The SCVA is the result of the following multi-component integration:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ SCVA_c = \frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot RW_c \cdot \sum_{NS \in c} (M_{NS} \cdot EAD_{NS} \cdot DF_{NS}) \]

</div>
