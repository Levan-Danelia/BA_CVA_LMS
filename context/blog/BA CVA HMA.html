<h1>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</h1>

<!-- SECTION 1: SUMMARY -->

<h2>Summary</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the transition to the <b>Full BA-CVA</b> framework, firms are encouraged to actively manage their risk through eligible credit hedges. However, regulatory capital relief is not granted without scrutiny. The <b>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</b> represents a critical technical component that penalizes basis risk—the inherent uncertainty that arises when a hedging instrument does not perfectly reference the same legal entity as the underlying exposure.</p>

</div>

<p>Under the PRA's near-final rules (PS9/24), the HMA acts as a "safety tax" on proxy hedges. This ensures that while risk mitigation is recognized, the potential for correlation breakdown during market stress is explicitly capitalized. In this section of our series, we break down the mathematical mechanics of the HMA and explore how sectoral and regional mismatches drive exponential capital add-ons.</p>

<!-- SECTION 2: CONCEPT & FORMULA -->

<h2>Understanding the HMA Concept</h2>

<p>In the world of credit risk management, the "perfect" hedge is a rare commodity. While buying protection on the exact legal entity you are exposed to (a direct match) is ideal, liquidity or availability constraints often force firms to use <b>proxy hedges</b>. These proxies might be parent companies, subsidiaries, or entities within the same industry sector.</p>

<p>The <b>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</b> is the regulator's way of accounting for the "basis risk" inherent in these proxies. It effectively acts as a <b>safety tax</b>: while the Single Name Hedge (SNH) reduces your capital by recognizing the protection purchased, the HMA adds back a portion of that capital to safeguard against the risk that the proxy hedge and the exposure might decouple during a credit event.</p>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>The calculation of HMA is prescriptive and focuses on the "mismatch" portion of your single-name hedges. The formula for the aggregate mismatch adjustment is:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ HMA = \sum_{h \in c} (1 - r_{hc}^2) \cdot (RW_h \cdot M_h^{SN} \cdot B_h^{SN} \cdot DF_h^{SN})^2 \]

</div>

<p>A crucial point for implementation is that the inputs for the HMA are <b>identical</b> to those used in the <span class="highlight-blue">Single Name Hedge (SNH)</span> calculation. This means once you have identified your eligible hedges and mapped their parameters, you are simply applying a different mathematical transformation to the same data set ($RW_h, M_h, B_h, DF_h$).</p>

<p>The use of a <b>squared term</b> for the risk component is a deliberate design choice by the PRA. Squaring the risk ensures that larger, more concentrated mismatching hedges attract <span class="highlight-red">exponentially higher penalties</span>, promoting a more precise hedging strategy.</p>

<hr>

<!-- SECTION 3: SUPERVISORY CORRELATION -->

<h2>Supervisory Correlation (\(r_{hc}\))</h2>

<p>The "engine" that drives the HMA is the <b>Supervisory Correlation (\(r_{hc}\))</b>. This parameter quantifies the regulator's confidence in how closely a hedging instrument's credit spread will move in tandem with the counterparty's credit spread. The PRA prescribes specific values based on the relationship between the hedge reference and the counterparty.</p>

<h3>Mapping the Relationship</h3>

<p>According to the <b>Credit Valuation Adjustment Risk Part (Rule 4.10)</b>, firms must assign \(r_{hc}\) based on the following hierarchy:</p>

<table>

<thead>

<tr>

<th>Hedge Type</th>

<th>Relationship to Counterparty \(c\)</th>

<th style="text-align: right;">Correlation Value (\(r_{hc}\))</th>

</tr>

</thead>

<tbody>

<tr>

<td><b>Direct Match</b></td>

<td>References counterparty \(c\) directly</td>

<td style="text-align: right;">100%</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td><b>Legally Related</b></td>

<td>References a parent or subsidiary of \(c\)</td>

<td style="text-align: right;">80%</td>

</tr>

<tr>

<td><b>Proxy Hedge</b></td>

<td>Shares the same sector and region as \(c\)</td>

<td style="text-align: right;">50%</td>

</tr>

</tbody>

</table>

<h3>The Mismatch Trigger</h3>

<p>The HMA formula relies on the term <span class="highlight-blue">\((1 - r_{hc}^2)\)</span>. This mathematical structure creates a clear binary outcome for capital requirements:</p>

<ul>

<li><b>Zero Mismatch (\(r_{hc} = 1.0\)):</b> If you have a direct match, the formula becomes \(1 - 1^2 = 0\). This results in an HMA of zero. The regulator considers this a "perfect" hedge.</li>

<li><b>Triggering the Add-on (\(r_{hc} < 1.0\)):</b> For all other cases, a mismatch arises where a portion of the hedge risk is added back:

<ul>

<li>A <b>Legally Related</b> hedge (\(0.8\)) results in a factor of \(0.36\).</li>

<li>A <b>Proxy Hedge</b> (\(0.5\)) results in a significant factor of \(0.75\).</li>

</ul>

</li>

</ul>

<h3>Portfolio Aggregation</h3>

<p>The aggregation of the Hedge Mismatch Adjustment is distinct from other BA-CVA components. While Standalone CVA or Reduced Approach calculations involve complex square-root formulas to account for diversification, the Portfolio HMA is a <b>linear summation</b> of the individual instrument charges.</p>

<p>This linear approach ensures that every imperfect hedge is penalized individually for its basis risk. In our portfolio example, the total HMA is derived by simply adding the results from our calculation table:</p>

<p style="text-align: center; font-family: 'Menlo', monospace; font-size: 1rem;">

HMA Total = 0.00 + 447.32 + 97.82 + 51.15 + 0.00

</p>

<div class="highlight-final-box">

Total Portfolio HMA = 596.29
