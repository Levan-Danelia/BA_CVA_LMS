<h1>Index Hedges</h1>

<!-- SUMMARY SECTION -->

<h2>Summary</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the "Full Approach" of the Basic Approach for CVA (BA-CVA), firms are permitted to recognize eligible hedging instruments to mitigate their own funds requirements. While single-name hedges target the idiosyncratic risks of specific counterparties, <b>Index Hedges (IH)</b> serve as a primary mitigant for the <b>systematic component</b> of counterparty credit spread risk. Under the PRA's PS9/24 framework, these broad-market instruments allow firms to offset market-wide volatility, provided they adhere to specific mapping rules and the mandatory 0.7 risk weight multiplier.</p>

</div>

<p>Effective 1 January 2026, the treatment of index hedges requires a precise understanding of sectoral mapping and the mathematical decomposition of risk. In this section, we examine the eligibility criteria for indices, the calculation of index risk weights, and the role of indices in reducing the systematic charge within the aggregate square-root formula.</p>

<!-- TECHNICAL MECHANICS SECTION -->

<h2>Index Hedges (IH)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>While Single Name Hedges (SNH) are utilized to mitigate name-specific idiosyncratic risk, <b>Index Hedges (IH)</b> are designed to offset the <span class="highlight-blue">systematic component</span> of CVA risk. By utilizing broad-market credit default swap indices (such as iTraxx Europe or CDX North America), firms can protect against general credit spread widening that impacts the entire portfolio simultaneously.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>The total value of index hedges for the portfolio is calculated as the sum of the risk-weighted, maturity-adjusted, and discounted notionals across all eligible index instruments:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ IH = \sum_{i} RW_i^{ind} \cdot M_i^{ind} \cdot B_i^{ind} \cdot DF_i^{ind} \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<p>To accurately compute the effectiveness of an index hedge, firms must break down the formula into four distinct regulatory parameters:</p>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Index Risk Weight (\(RW_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>This is the supervisory risk weight assigned to index hedge \(i\). It includes a mandatory <span class="highlight-blue">0.7 multiplier</span> to account for the basis risk between the index and the underlying portfolio counterparties.

</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Remaining Maturity (\(M_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The residual contractual life of the index hedging instrument, expressed in years. This parameter ensures that the duration of the protection purchased is appropriately matched against the duration of the systematic risk being hedged.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Hedge Notional (\(B_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The total notional amount of the eligible index hedge. This represents the gross amount of market protection purchased through the CDS index instrument.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Discount Factor (\(DF_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The supervisory discount factor for the index hedge, designed to reflect the present value of the risk protection. It is calculated using the standard supervisory rate of 5%.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- RISK WEIGHT SECTION -->

<h2>Index Risk Weight (RW<sub>i</sub><sup>ind</sup>)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>To determine the capital relief provided by an index hedge, firms must calculate a specific Index Risk Weight. This value is derived from a look-through of the index's underlying constituents to ensure the hedge appropriately reflects the credit quality of the protected market segment.</p>

</div>

<h3>How the Weight is Derived</h3>

<p>The regulatory weight for a credit index is not a fixed number. Instead, it is a derived value based on the actual composition of the index being used as a hedge. This ensures that the capital relief provided by the index is commensurate with the credit quality of its underlying names. The derivation follows a strict hierarchy: we first identify the sectoral risk weight for every name in the index (the "Look-Through"), calculate a single representative weight for the basket (the "Weighted Average"), and finally apply a safety buffer (the "0.7 Multiplier").</p>

<h3>Derivation Methodology</h3>

<p>The calculation of \(RW_i^{ind}\) follows a prescriptive 3-step process mandated by the PRA:</p>

<ol>

<li><b>Constituent Mapping:</b> Each name in the index is mapped to its relevant sector and credit quality from Table 1 to determine its standalone risk weight (\(RW_j\)).</li>

<li><b>Name-Weighted Aggregation:</b> The individual risk weights are aggregated based on their respective weights within the index to find the pre-scalar average.</li>

<li><b>The 0.70 Multiplier:</b> A mandatory scalar is applied to the weighted average to account for the basis risk between a broad index and specific portfolio counterparties.</li>

</ol>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ RW_i^{ind} = 0.7 \cdot \sum_{j \in \text{index}} (\text{Weight}_j \cdot RW_j) \]

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Deep Dive: The 0.7 Index Scalar</span>

<p>A fundamental principle of the BA-CVA is the distinction between market risk and name-specific risk. The <b>0.7 multiplier</b> applied to index risk weights reflects that index hedges are inherently less effective than single-name hedges. This scalar acts as a "basis risk buffer," acknowledging that the movements of a broad index will never perfectly track the specific credit spread volatility of an individual counterparty portfolio.</p>

<p>Furthermore, index hedges are strictly confined to the <span class="highlight-blue">Systematic</span> part of the final aggregation formula. Because an index represents a basket of names, it cannot provide protection against the Idiosyncratic risk of a specific counterparty default. This ensures that firms cannot use indices to artificially suppress the capital charges associated with name-specific concentrations.</p>

</div>

<h2>Index Hedge Maturity (M<sub>i</sub><sup>ind</sup>)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>The temporal dimension of a hedge is a critical driver of its capital-offset capability. Under the BA-CVA framework, the maturity of an index hedge must reflect its actual remaining contractual life, ensuring a duration match against the systematic credit spread risk residing in the portfolio. The parameter \(M_i^{ind}\) represents the remaining contractual maturity of the index eligible BA-CVA hedge, expressed in years. In the Basic Approach, this is a straightforward input based on the residual life of the CDS index instrument at the reporting date.</p>

<!-- DISCOUNTING SECTION -->

<h2>Index Hedge Discounting (DF<sub>i</sub><sup>ind</sup>)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>To align the value of the protection with the present value of the counterparty exposure, the framework applies a standardized supervisory discount factor. This factor ensures that long-dated hedges are appropriately weighted against the risk they mitigate. The discount factor is calculated using a prescriptive formula based on a flat supervisory discount rate of 5%.</p>

</div>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ DF_i^{ind} = \frac{1 - e^{-0.05 \cdot M_i^{ind}}}{0.05 \cdot M_i^{ind}} \]

</div>

<!-- NOTIONAL SECTION -->

<h2>Index Hedge Notional (B<sub>i</sub><sup>ind</sup>)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>The <b>Index Hedge Notional (\(B_i^{\text{ind}}\))</b> represents the gross amount of protection purchased through a credit default swap index. In the BA-CVA framework, this value serves as the base quantity that is adjusted by risk weights and discount factors to determine the total capital offset provided by the hedge.</p>

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">EAD vs. Notional: A Critical Distinction</span>

<p>It is vital for the reader to distinguish between the inputs used for counterparty exposure and those used for hedging instruments. On the <b>SCVA side</b> (the exposure side), the calculation relies on <span class="highlight-red">Exposure at Default (EAD)</span>, which is derived from the Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) framework and divided by a supervisory alpha (\(\alpha\)) to neutralize inherent conservatism. </p>

<p>In contrast, on the <b>Hedge side</b>, the regulation uses the pure <span class="highlight-blue">Notional Amount</span> of the instrument. Unlike EAD, which is a calculated metric of potential future loss, the hedge notional is the legally specified amount of protection purchased. For index credit default swaps, this is simply the contractual notional of the index basket.</p>

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Exemption from Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</span>

<p>A distinctive feature of the BA-CVA framework is that <span class="highlight-blue">Index Hedges are exempt from the Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</span>. While Single Name Hedges (SNH) attract a squared capital add-on (\(HMAC\)) for proxy basis risk, the aggregate Index Hedge value is applied directly within the systematic aggregation formula without a mismatch penalty.</p>

<p>This exclusion is based on two primary regulatory principles:</p>

<ol>

<li><b>The Input-Level Scalar:</b> The basis risk inherent in using a broad index to hedge specific counterparty exposures is already accounted for via the 0.7 multiplier. This mandatory haircut pre-charges for the lack of perfect correlation between the index and the underlying portfolio.</li>

<li><b>Systematic Nature:</b> HMA is specifically designed to penalize proxy basis risk for idiosyncratic exposures (name-specific risk). Since indices target the broad, systematic credit market rather than individual counterparty fortunes, the name-to-hedge correlation factor (\(r_{hc}\)) is not applicable, rendering the HMA mechanic redundant for these instruments.</li>

</ol>
