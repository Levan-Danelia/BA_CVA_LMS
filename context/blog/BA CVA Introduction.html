<!-- PART 2: INTRODUCTION -->

<h2>Introduction</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>The implementation of the <b>Basel 3.1 standards</b> results in significant modification of CVA methodology, as the regulatory environment shifts toward enhanced comparability and standardized risk weights. In this series, we will be focusing specifically on the <b>Basic Approach for CVA (BA-CVA)</b>.</p>

<p>A critical technical distinction to master is the difference between <b>Regulatory CVA</b> and <b>Accounting CVA</b>. While Accounting CVA (under IFRS 13) represents a fair value adjustment reflecting market-implied credit risk and internal P&L, Regulatory CVA is a supervisory proxy calculated using prescriptive formulas defined within the Rulebook. It is a standardized construct designed to capitalize counterparty credit spread risk rather than mirror a firm's internal fair value volatility. In the section below we will provide high level descriptions of key items.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 3: STANDALONE CVA -->

<h2>Standalone CVA (SCVA)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>The <b>Standalone CVA (\(SCVA_c\))</b> is the fundamental building block of the Basic Approach. It represents the regulatory capital requirement for a specific counterparty <i>c</i> before considering any portfolio-wide diversification or hedging benefits. In essence, it quantifies the credit risk of each counterparty in isolation.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>The PRA Rulebook defines the calculation of \(SCVA_c\) using the following prescriptive formula:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ SCVA_c = \frac{1}{\alpha} \cdot RW_c \cdot \sum_{NS \in c} (M_{NS} \cdot EAD_{NS} \cdot DF_{NS}) \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<p>To understand the mechanics of the SCVA, we must examine the five core components that drive the calculation:</p>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Alpha (\(\alpha\))</span>

<p>A supervisory scalar used to neutralize the conservatism inherent in Counterparty Credit Risk (CCR) exposure values. It is generally set at <b>1.4</b>, though it is reduced to <b>1.0</b> for specific entities like Pension Funds to provide capital relief.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Risk Weight (\(RW_c\))</span>

<p>A prescribed weight that reflects the volatility of a counterparty's credit spread. These weights are assigned based on the counterparty's sector and credit quality (e.g., Investment Grade vs. High Yield) as defined in the supervisory tables.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Effective Maturity (\(M_{NS}\))</span>

<p>The time horizon of the risk. A significant change in Basel 3.1 is the <b>removal of the 1-year maturity floor</b> for collateralized transactions in the BA-CVA, allowing for more risk-sensitive treatment of short-dated trades.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Exposure at Default (\(EAD_{NS}\))</span>

<p>The exposure value of the netting set, typically derived from the firm's existing CCR framework (such as SA-CCR or IMM). This value represents the potential loss if the counterparty were to default immediately.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Discount Factor (\(DF_{NS}\))</span>

<p>A factor used to discount the exposure to its present value. It is calculated using a flat supervisory discount rate of 5%, ensuring that long-dated exposures are weighted appropriately relative to immediate risks.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 4: K REDUCED -->

<h2>K Reduced</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>Once the Standalone CVA has been calculated for every counterparty in the portfolio, the next stage of the Basic Approach is aggregation. <b>\(K_{\text{reduced}}\)</b> represents the aggregate capital requirement for a firm under the assumption that no eligible CVA hedges are recognized. It is the "gross" view of the portfolio's counterparty credit spread risk.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Aggregation Formula</h3>

<p>The regulatory framework uses a square-root-of-sum-of-squares approach to combine individual risks. This formula relies on a fixed supervisory correlation parameter, <b>\(\rho = 0.5\)</b> (50%), to determine how risks interact across the portfolio:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ K_{\text{reduced}} = \sqrt{ \left( \rho \cdot \sum_{c} SCVA_c \right)^2 + (1 - \rho^2) \cdot \sum_{c} (SCVA_c)^2 } \]

</div>

<h3>Systematic vs. Unsystematic Risk</h3>

<p>The aggregation formula is mathematically designed to decompose CVA risk into two distinct orthogonal components. This ensures that the capital charge accounts for both market-wide trends and name-specific volatility.</p>

<div class="risk-section">

<span class="risk-title">1. Systematic Risk Component</span>

<p>The first term, \( (\rho \cdot \sum_{c} SCVA_c)^2 \), represents <span class="highlight-blue">Systematic Risk</span>. This component assumes that credit spreads across all counterparties are perfectly correlated. It captures the risk of a market-wide "spread blowout" where a macro-economic event causes spreads to widen for all participants simultaneously. Because it sums the SCVAs linearly before squaring, it does not provide any diversification benefit.</p>

</div>

<div class="risk-section">

<span class="risk-title">2. Unsystematic (Idiosyncratic) Risk Component</span>

<p>The second term, \( (1 - \rho^2) \cdot \sum_{c} (SCVA_c)^2 \), represents <span class="highlight-blue">Unsystematic or Idiosyncratic Risk</span>. This component captures the risk that is unique to a specific counterparty (e.g., a name-specific credit downgrade or bankruptcy). Unlike the systematic part, this term benefits from portfolio diversification; as you add more small exposures to the portfolio, the relative idiosyncratic risk contribution decreases.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 5: SINGLE NAME HEDGES -->

<h2>Single Name Hedges (SNH)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the "Full Approach" of BA-CVA, firms can recognize eligible hedging instruments to mitigate their counterparty credit spread risk. <b>Single Name Hedges (SNH)</b> are transactions—typically Credit Default Swaps (CDS)—used to offset the specific credit risk of a single counterparty. Unlike the gross view of K-Reduced, these hedges allow for a direct reduction of individual SCVA terms.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>For a given counterparty \(c\), the aggregate value of its single-name hedges (\(SNH_c\)) is calculated as follows:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ SNH_c = \sum_{h \in c} r_{hc} \cdot RW_h \cdot M_h^{SN} \cdot B_h^{SN} \cdot DF_h^{SN} \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<p>The effectiveness of a single-name hedge depends on its alignment with the underlying counterparty. The formula captures this through five parameters:</p>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Supervisory Correlation (\(r_{hc}\))</span>

<p>This factor determines how much of the hedge notional is recognized based on the relationship between the hedge reference name and the counterparty. The Rulebook prescribes three levels:

<ul>

<li><b>100%:</b> The hedge references counterparty \(c\) directly.</li>

<li><b>80%:</b> The hedge references an entity legally related to the counterparty (e.g., a parent or subsidiary).</li>

<li><b>50%:</b> The hedge references an entity in the same sector and region (proxy hedge).</li>

</ul>

</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Hedge Risk Weight (\(RW_h\))</span>

<p>The supervisory risk weight assigned to the reference name of the hedging instrument. This is derived from the same Table 1 used for counterparties, ensuring consistency between the risk being hedged and the instrument providing protection.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Hedge Notional (\(B_h^{SN}\))</span>

<p>The notional amount of the single-name eligible BA-CVA hedge. For contingent CDS, the notional must be determined by the current market value of the reference portfolio or instrument.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Remaining Maturity (\(M_h^{SN}\))</span>

<p>The residual contractual life of the hedging instrument. This ensures that the hedge duration is appropriately matched against the exposure duration calculated in the SCVA.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Discount Factor (\(DF_h^{SN}\))</span>

<p>The supervisory discount factor for the hedge, calculated using the standard 5% supervisory rate: \( \frac{1 - e^{-0.05 \cdot M_h^{SN}}}{0.05 \cdot M_h^{SN}} \). This ensures the present value of the hedge is comparable to the present value of the exposure.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 6: HEDGE MISMATCH ADJUSTMENT -->

<h2>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the "Full Approach," credit derivatives can significantly reduce capital requirements. However, the regulator recognizes that a hedge is only truly "perfect" if it references the exact same legal entity as the exposure. When a firm uses a proxy—such as a parent company CDS or a sector-matched entity—it introduces basis risk. The <b>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</b> is a mandatory capital add-on designed to penalize this lack of perfect correlation.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>The HMA is calculated as the sum of squared "hedge risks," weighted by a mismatch factor derived from the supervisory correlation (\(r_{hc}\)):</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ HMA = \sum_{h \in c} (1 - r_{hc}^2) \cdot (RW_h \cdot M_h^{SN} \cdot B_h^{SN} \cdot DF_h^{SN})^2 \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<p>The HMA essentially acts as a "safety tax" on imperfect insurance. Its impact is driven by two primary factors:</p>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">The Mismatch Factor \((1 - r_{hc}^2)\)</span>

<p>This multiplier determines the severity of the penalty based on the correlation defined in the SNH section:

<ul>

<li>If the hedge is a <b>Direct Match</b> (\(r_{hc} = 100\%\)): The factor becomes \(1 - 1^2 = 0\). The HMA is <b>zero</b>.</li>

<li>If the hedge is <b>Legally Related</b> (\(r_{hc} = 80\%\)): The factor is \(1 - 0.8^2 = 0.36\). You retain 36% of the hedge's risk as an add-on.</li>

<li>If the hedge is a <b>Sector/Region Proxy</b> (\(r_{hc} = 50\%\)): The factor is \(1 - 0.5^2 = 0.75\). You retain a significant <b>75%</b> of the risk as an add-on.</li>

</ul>

</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">The Squared Risk Term</span>

<p>The second part of the formula, \((RW_h \cdot M_h^{SN} \cdot B_h^{SN} \cdot DF_h^{SN})^2\), represents the standalone credit risk of the hedging instrument itself. Crucially, this term is <b>squared</b>. This mathematical structure ensures that larger mismatching hedges attract exponentially higher penalties than smaller ones, discouraging large-scale reliance on imperfect proxy hedges.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Scope (\(h \in c\))</span>

<p>The HMA is calculated individually for every single-name hedge \(h\) that is not a 100% match to its counterparty \(c\). These individual penalties are then summed to create the total portfolio-level mismatch adjustment.</p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 7: INDEX HEDGES -->

<h2>Index Hedges (IH)</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>While Single Name Hedges target specific idiosyncratic risks, <b>Index Hedges (IH)</b> are designed to mitigate the <span class="highlight-blue">systematic component</span> of CVA risk. By using credit default swap indices (such as iTraxx or CDX), firms can hedge against market-wide movements in credit spreads that affect multiple counterparties simultaneously.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Regulatory Formula</h3>

<p>The total value of index hedges is the sum of the risk-weighted and discounted notionals across all eligible index instruments:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ IH = \sum_{i} RW_i^{ind} \cdot M_i^{ind} \cdot B_i^{ind} \cdot DF_i^{ind} \]

</div>

<h3>Component Breakdown</h3>

<p>The calculation of index hedge effectiveness involves a specific look-through or mapping approach to determine the appropriate risk weight:</p>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Index Risk Weight (\(RW_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The supervisory risk weight of the index is derived from the standard Table 1 risk weights but includes a mandatory <span class="highlight-blue">0.7 multiplier</span> to account for the inherent basis risk between a broad index and a specific portfolio. The calculation depends on the index composition:

<ul>

<li><b>Homogeneous Index:</b> If all constituents belong to the same sector and credit quality, multiply the relevant Table 1 value by 0.7.</li>

<li><b>Heterogeneous Index:</b> If the index spans multiple sectors or qualities, calculate the name-weighted average of the Table 1 risk weights and then multiply by 0.7.</li>

</ul>

</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Hedge Notional (\(B_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The total notional amount of the index eligible BA-CVA hedge. This represents the total protection purchased through the index instrument.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Remaining Maturity (\(M_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The residual contractual life of the index hedge. Similar to single-name hedges, the duration of the protection is a critical factor in its capital-offset capability.</p>

</div>

<div class="component-item">

<span class="component-name">Discount Factor (\(DF_i^{ind}\))</span>

<p>The supervisory discount factor for the index hedge, calculated using the standard 5% supervisory rate: \( \frac{1 - e^{-0.05 \cdot M_i^{ind}}}{0.05 \cdot M_i^{ind}} \). </p>

</div>

<hr>

<!-- PART 8: K HEDGED -->

<h2>K Hedged</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the final stages of the Full BA-CVA approach, we move from analyzing individual hedges to calculating the <b>\(K_{\text{hedged}}\)</b>. This represents the total capital requirement for a portfolio where all eligible hedges—Single Name (SNH) and Index (IH)—are integrated. It is the mathematical "net" view of your CVA risk profile.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Portfolio Aggregation Formula</h3>

<p>The calculation of \(K_{\text{hedged}}\) builds upon the \(K_{\text{reduced}}\) structure but incorporates offsets and basis risk penalties. The formula is structured as follows:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ K_{\text{hedged}} = \sqrt{\left(\rho \cdot \sum_{c} (SCVA_c - SNH_c) - IH\right)^2 + (1 - \rho^2) \cdot \sum_{c} (SCVA_c - SNH_c)^2 + \sum_{c} HMA_c} \]

</div>

<h3>Systematic vs. Idiosyncratic Risk</h3>

<p>Just like the unhedged version, the regulatory formula splits risk into two components, but now considers the effect of hedges on each:</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">The Systematic Component</span>

<p>Represented by the term \( (\rho \cdot \sum (SCVA - SNH) - IH)^2 \). This captures the market-wide risk. Notice that both Single Name Hedges and <b>Index Hedges (IH)</b> are subtracted here. Because indices are broad market instruments, they are only permitted to reduce this systematic portion of the risk.</p>

</div>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">The Idiosyncratic Component</span>

<p>Represented by the term \( (1 - \rho^2) \cdot \sum (SCVA - SNH)^2 \). This captures the risk unique to specific counterparties. Crucially, <b>Index Hedges do not appear here</b>. An index cannot hedge the specific default or spread movement of a single company; therefore, only Single Name Hedges (SNH) can reduce this idiosyncratic risk.</p>

</div>

<h3>Why do we add back HMA?</h3>

<p>The term <span class="highlight-red">\(\sum HMA_c\)</span> sits at the end of the variance summation. As we explored in Part 6, the <b>Hedge Mismatch Adjustment (HMA)</b> is a penalty for basis risk. Even though a proxy hedge reduces the primary exposure, it introduces a <i>new</i> risk: the risk that the hedge and the exposure don't move together. The regulator requires this risk to be "added back" to the total capital charge to ensure that imperfect hedging doesn't result in an under-capitalized position.</p>

<h3>Why is IH only on the Systematic side?</h3>

<p>A fundamental principle of the BA-CVA is the distinction between market risk and name-specific risk:</p>

<ul>

<li><b>Index Hedges (IH)</b> are designed to track the general credit market. If the economy fails and all spreads widen, the index hedge pays out. This perfectly offsets the <b>Systematic</b> market movement.</li>

<li>However, if one specific company in your portfolio defaults while the rest of the market remains stable, a broad index will not provide a meaningful payout. Because indices lack "name-specific" sensitivity, the regulator correctly prevents them from offsetting <b>Idiosyncratic</b> risk.</li>

</ul>

<hr>

<!-- PART 9: K FULL -->

<h2>K Full</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>In the final step of the "Full Approach" calculation, the regulator brings together the unhedged and hedged components of the portfolio. <b>\(K_{\text{full}}\)</b> is the weighted average of the gross risk view (\(K_{\text{reduced}}\)) and the net risk view (\(K_{\text{hedged}}\)). This blending ensures that firms receive credit for risk mitigation while maintaining a prudent "backstop" against over-hedging.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Weighting Formula</h3>

<p>The calculation of \(K_{\text{full}}\) uses a fixed weighting parameter, <b>\(\beta\)</b>, to balance the two approaches:</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ K_{\text{full}} = \beta \cdot K_{\text{reduced}} + (1 - \beta) \cdot K_{\text{hedged}} \]

</div>

<h3>Understanding the \(\beta\) Parameter</h3>

<p>The \(\beta\) factor is a crucial calibration tool in the BA-CVA framework. According to the PRA Rulebook, this value is fixed at <b>25%</b> (\(\beta = 0.25\)).</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">The Role of \(\beta\): The Capital Floor</span>

<p>The \(\beta\) parameter acts as a <span class="highlight-blue">hedge recognition floor</span>. By assigning a 25% weight to the unhedged \(K_{\text{reduced}}\), the regulator ensures that hedges can only ever reduce the total CVA capital requirement by a maximum of 75%.</p>

<p>This means that even if a firm has a perfectly hedged portfolio where \(K_{\text{hedged}}\) is zero, the firm must still hold 25% of the original unhedged capital charge.</p>

</div>

<h3>Why is this weighting necessary?</h3>

<ul>

<li><b>Acknowledging Basis Risk:</b> No hedge is perfectly correlated in a crisis. The 25% weight recognizes that there is always residual risk that a hedge may fail to track the exposure precisely.</li>

<li><b>Preventing Over-Reliance:</b> It discourages firms from relying solely on complex hedging strategies to eliminate their capital requirements, ensuring they remain robustly capitalized for their primary counterparty exposures.</li>

<li><b>Model Conservatism:</b> Since the BA-CVA is a standardized approach with fixed correlations, the floor acts as a safeguard against potential underestimation of risk that might occur if full 100% hedge recognition were allowed.</li>

</ul>

<hr>

<!-- PART 10: K FINAL -->

<h2>K Final</h2>

<div class="main-intro">

<p>After navigating the complexities of standalone risk, systematic aggregation, and hedge recognition, we reach the final destination of the BA-CVA journey. <b>\(K_{\text{final}}\)</b> is the actual amount of own funds that an institution must hold against its CVA risk. This final figure is achieved by applying a global calibration parameter known as the <b>Discount Scalar</b>.</p>

</div>

<h3>The Final Capital Formula</h3>

<p>The total capital requirement is the product of the aggregate risk (\(K\)) and the supervisory discount scalar (\(DS_{\mathrm{BA-CVA}}\)):</p>

<div class="formula-box">

\[ \text{Capital Requirement} = DS_{\mathrm{BA-CVA}} \cdot K_{\mathrm{BA-CVA}} \]

</div>

<h3>The Role of the Discount Scalar (\(DS_{\mathrm{BA-CVA}}\))</h3>

<p>The Discount Scalar is a mandatory multiplier that calibrates the formulaic output of the Basic Approach to align with broader regulatory objectives. According to the PRA Rulebook (PS9/24), this value is fixed at <b>0.65</b>.</p>

<div class="tech-section">

<span class="tech-title">Why 0.65?</span>

<p>The application of a 0.65 multiplier essentially <span class="highlight-blue">discounts the calculated risk by 35%</span>. This calibration serves several critical functions:</p>

<ul>

<li><b>International Alignment:</b> It aligns the UK's capital outcomes with the Basel 3.1 standards, ensuring that UK firms are not disproportionately penalized compared to international peers.</li>

<li><b>Consistency with SA-CVA:</b> Since the BA-CVA uses prescriptive, often conservative assumptions (like the fixed 50% correlation), the scalar ensures that the resulting capital is not punitively higher than what a firm would calculate using the more sophisticated Standardised Approach (SA-CVA).</li>

<li><b>Preventing Capital Cliffs:</b> It helps smooth the transition from the legacy CRR framework to the new regime, mitigating the impact of scope expansion (such as the inclusion of SFTs) on the industry's total capital requirements.</li>

</ul>

</div>
